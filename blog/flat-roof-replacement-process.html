The Flat Roof Replacement Process: What to Expect

Updated April 2026 · 8 min read

A flat roof replacement is a significant investment for any property owner. Understanding each step of the process helps you plan effectively, ask the right questions, and avoid costly surprises. This guide walks you through the entire flat roof replacement process from initial inspection to final warranty documentation.

Step 1: Roof Inspection and Assessment

Every flat roof replacement begins with a thorough inspection. A qualified roofing contractor will examine your existing roof to assess its current condition, identify the extent of damage, and determine whether a full replacement is necessary or if targeted repairs could extend the roof's life.

During the inspection, the contractor will check for ponding water, membrane deterioration, flashing failures, insulation moisture damage, and structural issues with the roof deck. Many contractors use infrared moisture scanning to detect trapped moisture beneath the membrane that is invisible to the naked eye. This information is critical for determining the full scope of work required.

Step 2: Getting Multiple Quotes

Once you understand the condition of your roof, the next step is to collect detailed quotes from at least three qualified flat roofing contractors. Each quote should include a complete scope of work covering the membrane type, insulation specifications, tear-off or overlay approach, flashing details, drainage improvements, and warranty terms.

When comparing quotes, do not simply choose the lowest number. Look for detailed line-item breakdowns that clearly separate material costs, labour, disposal fees, and permit costs. A vague lump-sum quote is often a red flag. Ask each contractor to explain their approach and why they recommend a particular roofing system for your building.

Step 3: Choosing Your Roofing Material

The material you select will affect the project cost, timeline, and long-term performance of your new roof. The most common flat roofing options in Canada include TPO, EPDM, PVC, modified bitumen, and built-up roofing (BUR).

Step 4: Planning the Timeline

A typical flat roof replacement for a small to mid-size commercial building takes between three and ten working days, depending on the roof size, material choice, weather conditions, and complexity of the project. Residential flat roofs on homes or duplexes can often be completed in one to three days.

Your contractor should provide a detailed project schedule that accounts for material delivery lead times, permit approval, weather contingencies, and any coordination needed with other trades. In Canada, the optimal roofing season runs from May through October, though many materials can be installed in cooler weather with proper precautions.

Step 5: Tear-Off vs. Overlay

One of the most important decisions in a flat roof replacement is whether to perform a full tear-off or an overlay. Each approach has distinct advantages and trade-offs.

Full Tear-Off

A full tear-off involves removing the existing roofing membrane, insulation, and sometimes the vapour barrier down to the structural roof deck. This allows the contractor to inspect the deck for rot, rust, or structural damage and replace any compromised components before installing the new system. Tear-off is required if the existing roof has multiple layers, if there is trapped moisture in the insulation, or if the deck needs repair. The additional labour and disposal costs typically add $1.00 to $2.50 per square foot to the project.

Overlay (Re-Cover)

An overlay involves installing a new roofing membrane directly over the existing one. This option is faster, less expensive, and produces less waste. However, it is only viable if the existing roof has a single layer, the insulation is dry, and the deck is structurally sound. Most building codes limit a flat roof to a maximum of two membrane layers. An overlay does carry the risk of trapping existing moisture problems beneath the new membrane.

Your contractor should clearly explain which approach they recommend and why. If a contractor pushes for an overlay without performing a thorough moisture assessment, consider that a warning sign.

Step 6: Installation Day

On installation day, the roofing crew will arrive with all materials and equipment. Here is what to expect during the actual work.

  1. The crew sets up safety equipment, ladders, and material staging areas on the ground and roof
  2. If doing a tear-off, the old membrane, insulation, and debris are removed and loaded into disposal bins
  3. The roof deck is inspected and any damaged sections are repaired or replaced
  4. A new vapour barrier is installed if required by building code or the roofing system specification
  5. New insulation boards are laid and mechanically fastened or adhered to the deck
  6. The new roofing membrane is rolled out, positioned, and secured using the method specified for that material (heat-welded, adhered, or mechanically attached)
  7. All seams are sealed, and flashing is installed around every penetration, edge, and transition point
  8. Drains, scuppers, and other drainage components are connected and tested
  9. The crew performs a final walkthrough and cleanup of the site

During installation, expect noise from equipment, increased traffic around your building, and temporary interruption to rooftop HVAC systems. A professional contractor will communicate the schedule clearly and minimize disruption to your business operations.

Step 7: Inspections and Quality Checks

After installation is complete, several inspections should take place to verify the quality of the work.

  • Contractor quality check: The project manager or foreman should conduct a thorough inspection of all seams, flashings, penetrations, and drainage points before the crew leaves the site
  • Municipal building inspection: Most Canadian municipalities require a building inspection for roof replacement projects. The inspector verifies that the installation meets local building code requirements, including insulation R-values and fire ratings
  • Manufacturer inspection: If the contractor is providing a manufacturer-backed warranty (such as an NDL warranty from GAF, Carlisle, or Firestone), the manufacturer may send their own inspector to verify that the installation meets their specifications
  • Owner walkthrough: You or your property manager should walk the roof with the contractor to review the finished work, ask questions, and document the condition of the new roof with photos

Do not sign off on the project or release final payment until all required inspections have been completed and any deficiencies have been corrected.

Step 8: Warranty Documentation

A properly completed flat roof replacement should come with two types of warranty coverage.

Workmanship Warranty

This warranty is provided by the roofing contractor and covers defects in installation. Typical workmanship warranties range from 2 to 10 years. A contractor who stands behind their work should offer at least a 5-year workmanship warranty. Make sure you receive this warranty in writing with clear terms about what is and is not covered.

Manufacturer Material Warranty

The membrane manufacturer provides this warranty, which covers defects in the roofing material itself. Standard material warranties range from 15 to 30 years depending on the product and membrane thickness. Premium NDL (No Dollar Limit) warranties cover both materials and labour for repairs during the warranty period and are the gold standard for commercial flat roofs.

Keep all warranty documents, invoices, inspection reports, and maintenance records in a safe place. Many warranties require regular maintenance and inspections to remain valid, so establish a maintenance schedule with your contractor before the project is complete.

How Long Does the Entire Process Take?

From your first phone call to a completed, warrantied roof, the entire process typically takes four to eight weeks. The breakdown looks roughly like this: one to two weeks for inspections and quotes, one to two weeks for material ordering and permit approval, and one to two weeks for the actual installation work. Complex projects or supply chain delays can extend this timeline.
