How to Choose a Flat Roof Contractor in Canada

Updated April 2026 · 8 min read

Hiring the wrong roofing contractor is one of the most expensive mistakes a building owner can make. A flat roof installed by an unqualified crew can fail in just a few years, voiding manufacturer warranties and costing you tens of thousands in premature replacement. This guide covers exactly what to look for and what to avoid when choosing a flat roof contractor in Canada.

1. Verify Licensing and Insurance

Before you even discuss pricing, confirm that the contractor is properly licensed and insured. Requirements vary by province, but the essentials are consistent across Canada.

What to check

  • Business licence: Confirm the contractor holds a valid licence in your municipality. In Ontario, check for registration with the Ontario College of Trades (now part of Skilled Trades Ontario).
  • WSIB coverage: In Ontario, contractors must have Workplace Safety and Insurance Board (WSIB) clearance. Without it, you could be liable for worker injuries on your property.
  • Liability insurance: Ask for a Certificate of Insurance showing a minimum of $2 million in commercial general liability coverage. Verify the certificate directly with the insurance company.
  • Vehicle and equipment insurance: Ensure any vehicles and equipment brought to your site are properly insured.

2. Look for Manufacturer Certifications

The most reliable flat roof contractors hold certifications from the membrane manufacturers whose products they install. These certifications mean the contractor has been trained, audited, and approved by the manufacturer to install their systems.

Common manufacturer certification programs include:

  • Carlisle SynTec: Authorized Applicator and ESP (Exceptional Seam Performance) programs
  • Firestone Building Products: Certified and Master Contractor programs
  • GAF: Master Select and TopField programs
  • Sika Sarnafil: Registered and Qualified Applicator programs
  • Johns Manville: Peak Advantage Contractor program

Manufacturer-certified contractors can offer enhanced warranty packages, including coverage for both materials and workmanship, that non-certified installers cannot provide.

3. Evaluate Their Flat Roof Experience

Flat roofing is a specialized trade. A contractor who primarily installs shingles on residential homes may not have the skills needed for a commercial flat roof installation. Ask specifically about their experience with the membrane type you are considering.

  • How many flat roof projects have they completed in the past 12 months?
  • Can they provide photos or references for projects of similar size and material?
  • How long have they been installing the specific membrane you are considering?
  • Do their crews specialize in flat roofing, or do they split time between flat and sloped work?

4. Understand Warranty Offerings

Flat roof warranties can be confusing. There are typically two separate warranties involved: the manufacturer's material warranty and the contractor's workmanship warranty. The best contractors offer both, often through a manufacturer-backed system warranty that covers everything.

A No Dollar Limit (NDL) warranty from a major manufacturer is the gold standard. It means the manufacturer will cover the full cost of repairs, including labour, with no cap. Only certified contractors can offer NDL warranties, which is another reason to prioritize manufacturer certification.

5. Get Multiple Quotes

Always get at least three written quotes from different contractors. This helps you understand the fair market price for your project and identify outliers that may indicate cut corners or inflated pricing.

  • Ensure every quote covers the same scope of work (membrane type, insulation, tear-off, flashings, drainage)
  • Ask for line-item pricing so you can compare material costs, labour, and disposal separately
  • Be wary of quotes that are dramatically lower than others, as this often indicates the contractor is cutting corners on materials, skipping tear-off, or planning to use unlicensed subcontractors
  • Do not automatically choose the lowest bid; value and quality matter more over a 20-year roof lifecycle

6. Check References and Past Work

A reputable contractor will happily provide references from recent projects. Go beyond the names they give you and do your own research as well.

  • Call at least two references and ask about timeline adherence, worksite cleanliness, and post-project support
  • Check Google Reviews, Better Business Bureau (BBB) ratings, and HomeStars (in Canada)
  • Ask if you can visit a recently completed project to see the workmanship firsthand
  • Look for complaints with your provincial consumer protection office

7. Understand the Scope of Work

Before signing a contract, make sure you fully understand what is included and what is not. A clear, detailed scope of work protects both you and the contractor.

The contract should specify:

  • Exact membrane type, manufacturer, thickness, and colour
  • Insulation type, thickness, and R-value
  • Whether the existing roof will be torn off or overlaid
  • Flashing and edge detail specifications
  • Drainage modifications, if any
  • Cleanup and disposal procedures
  • Permit responsibilities
  • Payment schedule (never pay more than 10–15% upfront)
  • Start and completion dates with delay provisions

8. Red Flags to Watch For

Knowing what to avoid is just as important as knowing what to look for. Walk away from any contractor who exhibits these warning signs:

  • Demands large deposits upfront: A request for more than 10–15% upfront is a red flag. Reputable contractors have supplier accounts and do not need your money to buy materials.
  • No written contract: Any contractor unwilling to provide a detailed written contract is not someone you want on your roof.
  • Pressure to sign immediately: High-pressure sales tactics like "this price is only good today" are a sign of a company that relies on impulse rather than reputation.
  • Cannot provide proof of insurance: If they cannot produce a valid Certificate of Insurance within 24 hours, move on.
  • No physical address: Contractors operating solely from a phone number and P.O. Box can disappear when warranty claims arise.
  • Unusually low bid: If one bid is 30% or more below the others, the contractor is likely cutting corners somewhere critical.
